Remark. This representation avoids cancellation of leading digits in the numerical evaluation, if  $ 1 - \rho(t) \to 0^{+} $.

By definition, one has

 $$ \mu_{1}\geq\mu_{0}\quad, $$ 

which implies

 $$ \rho\geq0. $$ 

Note that equality in (3.16) and (3.16') holds, if and only if

 $$ P_{s}=P_{1}\cdot\delta_{s,1}\quad. $$ 

In the limiting case  $ \rho = 0 $, the weight function (3.2) degenerates to the discrete distribution

 $$ \Psi(s)=\delta_{s,1}\quad. $$ 

For the variation of  $ \rho $, one needs the relation

 $$ \frac{\partial l_{k}}{\partial\rho}=\frac{k}{1-\rho}\left(l_{k-1}-l_{k}\right)\;. $$ 

The discrete Laguerre polynomials permit a natural extension to the heterogeneous case — which will be presented in a forthcoming paper.

### 3.2 Charlier Polynomials

Consider the Poisson distribution

 $$ \Psi(s):=e^{-\lambda}\cdot\frac{\lambda^{s-1}}{(s-1)!}\quad,\quad s=1,2,\ldots $$ 

with normalization (2.24). The associated discrete inner product (2.1) generates a set of orthogonal polynomials — the so-called Charlier polynomials  $ \{c_k(s)\} $, which have already been treated by CHARLIER [3] in 1905.

Their three-term recurrence relation is

 $$ \lambda c_{k+1}=\left(k+\lambda+1-s\right)c_{k}-k c_{k-1} $$ 

to be started with

 $$ c_{-1}:=0,\quad c_{0}:=1. $$
