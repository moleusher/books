## Abstract

This paper presents an LLM agent called AgentCAT that extracts and analyzes catalytic reaction data from chemical engineering papers. The system aims to overcome the long-standing data bottleneck in chemical engineering by providing natural-language-based interactive data analysis. The authors formalize an abstraction of the catalytic reaction data extraction task in an AI-friendly manner. The complex catalytic process creates complicated dependency structures across elementary reaction steps, molecular behaviors, and measurement evidence. AgentCAT makes four technical contributions: (1) a schema-governed extraction pipeline with progressive schema evolution; (2) a dependency-aware reaction-network knowledge graph linking catalysts, active sites, synthesis-derived descriptors, mechanistic claims with evidence, and macroscopic outcomes; (3) a general querying module for natural-language exploration and visualization; and (4) an evaluation on roughly 800 peer-reviewed chemical engineering publications.

## 1. Introduction

Chemical engineering emphasizes engineering practicability and industrial scalability, distinguishing it from pure chemical research focused on reaction mechanisms. The core goal involves translating lab-scale feasibility into industrial production. Experiments at various scales generate catalytic reaction data that reveals insights for optimizing catalytic processes.

The scarcity of catalytic reaction data has been a persistent bottleneck in chemical engineering. Public datasets like ORD, Orderly, and DigCat exist for broader chemistry, but they typically represent reactions as isolated tuples offering limited insight for chemical engineering, where catalytic processes and reaction environments distinguish the data. General-purpose LLMs such as Gemini and Grok show promise for extracting such data from literature.

Figure 1 shows a graph abstraction of catalytic reaction data. The top layer represents a catalyst—molecular sieves like HZSM-5 exhibit complexity from hierarchical structural features and dynamic behaviors. Each catalyst has active sites where elementary reaction steps occur. The catalytic process can be described at micro and macro levels, with evidence generated through techniques like probe molecular and pyridine-adsorbed IR spectra.

The authors note that "general-purpose LLMs such as Gemini and Grok excel in semantic understanding and multimodal reasoning," but their direct application to extracting catalyst experimental data often falls short in ensuring causal consistency and verifiable grounding. Existing scientific data extraction systems like SLM-MATRIX, Eunomia, and nanoMINER cannot handle the complex dependency structures in catalytic reaction data.

AgentCAT adopts a schema-governed, closed-loop design. The system makes extraction targets explicit via progressive schema evolution, grounds each field in evidence through staged extraction and self-correction, and organizes records into a dependency-aware knowledge graph.

**Contributions:**
- Formulation of chemical-engineering catalyst experiment extraction as reconstructing an SSP-oriented, process-coupled "partial picture," identifying three failure modes of general LLMs
- A multi-agent, plan-then-execute pipeline with progressive schema evolution and evidence-grounded extraction
- A reaction-network knowledge graph in Neo4j linking catalysts, active sites, descriptors, mechanistic claims, and outcomes
- Evaluation on roughly 800 peer-reviewed publications

## 2. Related Work

### Chemistry Agents
Several LLM-based chemistry agents have emerged. ChemCrow integrates an LLM with chemistry tools for multi-step problem solving. CACTUS connects LLMs to cheminformatics tool chains. ChemAgent scales up tool-augmented frameworks. Agentic systems for scientific information extraction include SLM-MATRIX, Eunomia, and nanoMINER. OpenChemIE provides a toolkit for document-level chemistry literature extraction. However, these systems cannot handle the complex dependency structure in catalytic reaction data.

### Chemistry Knowledge Graphs
CKGs provide structured representations of chemistry entities and reactions. Domain experts manually curate resources like BioGRID, achieving high precision but limited scalability. Rule-based or shallow model methods enable rapid processing but struggle with complex semantics. LLM-based methods leverage contextual understanding for automated extraction, though most remain restricted to specific domains.

### LLM-based Knowledge Graph Construction
Approaches like Microsoft's GraphRAG extract triples but often produce sparse graphs. KGGen combines LLM-driven extraction with post-processing. Agentic frameworks enable autonomous planning and self-correction. However, these methods lack strong domain-specific prior knowledge and struggle with global consistency.

## 3. Preliminary & Challenges

### 3.1 Graph Abstraction of Catalytic Reaction Data

In chemical engineering, reactions are tightly coupled by the catalytic process. Experimental facts are interpretable only when linked along this process. Techniques like probe molecular and IR spectra generate evidence forming a "partial picture" of the catalytic process.

Figure 2 presents a graph abstraction using the Methanol-to-Olefins (MTO) reaction as an example. It represents the Synthesis–Structure–Performance (SSP) paradigm: "synthesis protocols determine physicochemical structures, which in turn constrain mechanistic pathways and ultimately manifest as observable performance metrics."

**Dependency on material synthesis.** Many factors influence catalytic processes. For example, in H-ZSM-5 zeolites, changing the Si/Al ratio alters acidity-related descriptors, affecting hydrocarbon-pool evolution. Extracting a metric like "ethene selectivity" is incomplete unless anchored to the catalyst identity and its structural descriptors.

**Mechanism-driven complexity.** A catalytic reaction encapsulates both macroscopic outcomes and microscopic mechanistic events governed by active sites and pore environments. Acidity factors modulate hydrogen-transfer reactivity and the buildup of aromatic species.

**Evidence of the catalytic process.** Mechanistic links are supported by heterogeneous evidence, such as ¹³C/¹²C methanol switching experiments and co-feeding experiments.

**Heterogeneity in experimental design.** Outcomes are highly sensitive to design choices. Even within one paper, the same reaction can be studied under different regimes—pulse micro-reactors for transient probing versus continuous-flow reactors for steady-state evaluation. Extracted measurements must be contextualized by reactor mode, temporal regime, and associated protocols.

The authors conclude that an effective extraction system "must therefore recover mechanistic claims together with their supporting evidence and experimental context."

### 3.2 Challenges for General LLMs

Through a case study on H-ZSM-5 catalysis, the authors identify three recurring failure modes:

1. **Lost-in-the-middle in causal linking.** Dependent information appears across distant sections and modalities. Standard LLMs frequently break these dependencies or conflate attributes.
2. **Hallucination in fine-grained parameter alignment.** Many quantities are protocol- and time-indexed measurements. When extracted without preserving experimental context, numbers lose physical meaning.
3. **Lack of domain logic constraints.** Catalyst experiment data must satisfy domain constraints. "A free-form LLM has no built-in mechanism to enforce these constraints or to detect violations."

## 4. The Design of AgentCAT

### 4.1 Architectural Overview

AgentCAT is a multi-agent orchestration framework built on two principles: plan-then-execute and closed-loop self-correction. The system decouples the workflow into schema specification, evidence collection, and verification. Feedback loops are embedded at every layer.

As shown in Figure 3, the design progresses through three stages: Adaptive Extraction, Knowledge Graph Construction, and Interactive Graph Exploration. These target the three failure modes: progressive schema evolution preserves long-range links; candidate-then-resolve evidence grounding improves parameter alignment; and review-driven re-extraction enforces domain-logic consistency.

### 4.2 Adaptive Information Extraction

The core extraction pipeline is orchestrated by LangGraph.

**Progressive schema evolution.** Rather than relying on manually designed static schemas, AgentCAT uses a Human-in-the-Loop Planning mechanism. A Planning Agent collaborates with researchers to establish preliminary DomainKeyElements. A Schema-Evolution Agent then iteratively processes a stratified sample of papers, refining the schema while ensuring backward compatibility.

**Two-phase evidence-based extraction.** The schema is partitioned into segments processed through a "Candidate-then-Resolve" strategy. Phase 1 scans the document section by section to extract verbatim text fragments as potential evidence. Phase 2 processes these candidates alongside their original context to populate structured fields.

**Review and quality verdict.** A review agent assigns one of three verdicts: PASS, MINOR_FIX, or MAJOR_ERROR. For MAJOR_ERROR, a re-extraction loop is triggered with error-reason hints injected into the prompt.

### 4.3 Knowledge Graph Construction

The Representation Module transforms structured JSON into a Reaction Network Graph stored in Neo4j. It models relationships among catalyst properties, microscopic molecular adsorption, and macroscopic catalytic processes.

**Dynamic labeling management.** A Label Manager supports dynamic schema expansion under a conservative policy, introducing new labels only when strictly necessary to preserve structural coherence.

**Entity normalization and linking.** A Molecular Normalizer standardizes chemical formulas and resolves naming discrepancies. Essential nodes are linked to source PDF identifiers for traceability.

### 4.4 General Querying and Graph Exploration

A General-Querying Agent uses a natural language interface, enabling researchers to interact with the reaction network intuitively. The agent progressively decomposes complex queries into plans, executes sub-queries, synthesizes results, and revises plans as needed.

The querying agent integrates with a graph visualization interface that unifies catalytic mechanisms from multiple publications into a single visual canvas, enabling cross-document exploration.

## 5. Performance Evaluation

### 5.1 Evaluation Settings

**Dataset.** Roughly 800 peer-reviewed publications from the energy catalysis domain, curated and validated by domain experts.

**Implementation details.** All agents use LangGraph. Adaptive information extraction employs Gemini-2.5-Pro via Google AI Studio. Knowledge graph construction and querying use doubao-seed-1.8 via VolcanoArk. The graph is persisted in Neo4j.

### 5.2 Performance of Schema Evolution

Using 10 representative PDFs starting from an empty schema, the system was evaluated on newly induced items per round. Figure 4 reveals a bootstrapping-then-convergence pattern: "the initial round establishes the core structure, while subsequent rounds contribute only marginal extensions."

### 5.3 Performance of Data Extracting

**Chemical engineering expert evaluation.** Three experts evaluated extraction from 20 randomly selected papers across Completeness, Accuracy, and Readability (1–10 scale). Figure 5(a) shows that Accuracy and Readability are consistently strong, while Completeness shows modest variation.

**Computing-level performance.** Figure 5(b) reports JSON output lengths for 733 papers. Figure 5(c) shows review verdicts across 4,398 extracted sections: "The pipeline achieves an 82.0% PASS rate, with 15.3% MINOR_FIX and only 2.7% MAJOR_ERROR." The low incidence of major failures supports the value of review-driven gating.

### 5.4 Performance of General Querying Agent

A benchmark of 12 queries across three difficulty levels (Easy, Medium, Hard) was used, with each query executed five times (60 trials total). Results in Table 2 show an overall correctness rate of 86.67%. Easy and Medium queries had 10% error rates, while Hard queries reached 20%. Execution time increased with difficulty, with Hard queries averaging 308.32 seconds.

## 6. Conclusion

The paper presents AgentCAT for extracting and analyzing catalytic reaction data from chemical engineering literature. The system serves as an alternative to address the long-standing data bottleneck in the field. AgentCAT provides a formal abstraction and challenge analysis of the extraction task. The four technical contributions are a schema-governed extraction pipeline, a dependency-aware knowledge graph, a general querying module, and evaluation on roughly 800 peer-reviewed papers demonstrating effectiveness.

---

## 7. Limitations and Ethical Considerations

**Ethical considerations.** The work analyzes publicly available, peer-reviewed publications and does not involve human participants. AgentCAT extracts structured experimental facts with provenance signals but does not redistribute copyrighted text. Automated extraction may contain errors; "outputs should be treated as decision support rather than ground truth."

**Limitations.** Interactive querying can be time-consuming due to multi-step planning. Some fine-grained misalignment may persist in challenging cases. Evaluation focuses on energy-catalysis; generalization to other subdomains may require further schema refinement.
